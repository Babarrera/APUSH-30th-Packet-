1. Page 399-400. List and describe 3 different "tolls" on the environment from this era.

  • Destruction of animal species: The number of grizzly bears and wolves was drastically reduced. The buffalo was almost entirely eliminated, with the population plummeting from millions to only about 5,000 by the mid-1880s.
  • Destruction of grasslands: After the buffalo were killed off, ranchers shifted to cattle and sheep, which devoured the native grasses down to the roots. This destroyed the ground cover, leading to soil erosion and massive dust storms.
  • Destruction of inland waters: Farmers dammed and rerouted water for irrigation, causing many bodies of water like Lake Tulare to shrink dramatically or disappear entirely. This also caused the water table to drop and left some land too alkaline for farming.

2. What were 2 separate government actions taken as a result of this?

  • The Forest Reserve Act of 1891, which gave the president the power to establish forest reserves to protect watersheds.
  • The Newlands (National Reclamation) Act of 1902, which provided federal funding for irrigation projects like dams and canals.

3. Page 476. Who cares more about the environment...Theodore Roosevelt? Or John Muir? Why?
John Muir cares more about the environment for its own sake. The text explains that Muir was a preservationist who made a "passionate and spiritual defense of the inherent value of the American wilderness," seeing it as a "temple." In contrast, Roosevelt was a conservationist who believed in managing natural resources for public use, which led him to support the damming of the Hetch Hetchy Valley for a water reservoir.

4. What would a conservationist say to a preservationist if they argued over land use?
A conservationist would say: "We should use this land wisely and efficiently for the greatest good for the greatest number of people. Building this dam here will provide clean drinking water and electricity for the city, which is a victory for the public good."

A preservationist would reply: "This valley is a sacred temple of nature, with an inherent value that transcends its usefulness to humans. To flood it for commercial and urban purposes would be an act of destruction driven by 'ravaging commercialism.' It must be left in its natural, untouched state."

5. 21.6.5 What grade would you give Woodrow Wilson on his first term? What do you agree with or disagree with in his presidency?
Grade: B

Agree with:

  • Economic Reform: I agree with his efforts to reduce tariffs on essential goods (Underwood-Simmons Act) and his establishment of the Federal Reserve to create a more stable and central banking system.
  • Antitrust and Labor: I support the Clayton Antitrust Act for exempting unions from being considered illegal trusts and for protecting them from unfair injunctions, which was a major win for the labor movement.
  • Child Labor: I agree with the Keating-Owen Act, as banning child labor was a crucial social justice reform.

Disagree with:

  • Social Issues: I strongly disagree with his segregationist policies. Issuing an executive order to segregate the federal workforce was a regressive and harmful act that institutionalized racism in the government.
  • Caution on Reform: His initial reluctance to support child labor laws and aid to farmers showed a cautiousness that put him behind the more progressive wing of his party.

1. Problem and alternative, and how Jefferson/Hamilton apply in the 20th century: The problem was that corporations were becoming national/international and controlling people's lives. The alternative was a strong national government. Herbert Croly argued that to achieve the Jeffersonian ideal of individual freedom, the country needed Hamiltonian government intervention in the economy.

2. Teddy's Square Deal aims AND federal government's responsibility: The Square Deal aimed to distinguish good corporations (useful products at fair prices) from bad ones (existing only to profit). Roosevelt believed the federal government was responsible for regulating the economy directly and breaking up the power of wealthy corporations.

3. Actions Teddy took AND how Congress assisted: Teddy used the Sherman Act to prosecute bad trusts (like Northern Securities Company) and appointed Gifford Pinchot to set aside millions of acres for national parks. Congress passed the Hepburn Act (1906), giving the ICC power to regulate railroad rates.

4. What Taft accomplished as a Progressive: Taft ordered the prosecution that broke up Standard Oil (1911) and supported the 16th Amendment allowing a federal income tax.

5. Four candidates and platforms in 1912:

  • Taft (Republican) – conservative, limited trust-busting
  • Roosevelt (Bull Moose/Progressive) – New Nationalism; heavy taxes on wealth, federal regulation, welfare state programs
  • Debs (Socialist) – public ownership of railroads/banks, limits on work hours
  • Wilson (Democrat) – New Freedom; restore market competition, stronger anti-trust laws, support small business

6. Problems according to the Mystery Document (Wilson): Excessive tariffs and near-universal monopoly, and that both wings of the Republican Party refused to fight these problems, accepting them instead through administrative commissions.

7. Four of Wilson's domestic accomplishments:

  • Underwood Tariff reducing import duties
  • Graduated income tax on the wealthiest 5%
  • Clayton Act (1914) exempting unions from antitrust laws
  • Keating-Owen Act outlawing child labor in manufacturing
  • (Also: Adamson Act — 8-hour workday for railroad workers; Federal Reserve System founded 1913)

8. The "contradiction" with Progressive presidents: Progressive presidents were very concerned about big business threatening freedom at home, but in Latin America and the Caribbean they showed little concern for freedom at all, frequently intervening militarily to benefit American business interests.

9. What the Roosevelt Corollary empowered the U.S. to do AND how Teddy put it into practice: It added to the Monroe Doctrine by declaring the U.S. would exercise "international police power" over the Western Hemisphere. In practice, Teddy used troops to seize the Dominican Republic's customs house to ensure debt repayment, and encouraged United Fruit Company investment in Honduras and Costa Rica, turning them into "banana republics."

10. Contrast Taft's foreign policy with Wilson's: Taft used "Dollar Diplomacy" — emphasizing loans and economic investment rather than military force to spread American influence. Wilson, despite appearing more idealistic, actually intervened militarily in Latin America more than any other president, sending troops to Mexico to "teach" them democracy, though these interventions largely failed.

11. Foner Quote: "The presidents who spoke the most about freedom were likely to intervene most frequently in the affairs of other countries."

🌲 Guided notes — conservation vs. preservation

Background Info
What is conservation? Advocating the proper use of nature and resources
- Example: Teddy Roosevelt / Gifford Pinchot — Roosevelt's chief forester

What is preservation? Advocating the protection of nature and land from humans
- Example: John Muir (Sierra Club)

Similarities Between The Two Groups

  • Both conservationists and preservationists advocated establishing national parks
  • Yellowstone in 1872 — 1st national park
  • Yosemite in 1890

Differences Between The Two Groups

  • Conservationists support using nature
  • Preservationists wanted to protect nature from human interaction

The Hetch Hetchy Controversy

  • Hetch Hetchy is in Yosemite National Park
  • San Francisco sought a stable water supply — proposed a dam
  • John Muir (Sierra Club and preservationist) opposed the dam
  • Pinchot (and Roosevelt) approved construction of the dam
  • 85% of water usage today in San Fran comes from Hetch Hetchy, 167 miles away

Test Tips

  • Multiple-Choice and Short Answer Questions: Comparing and contrasting preservation and conservation / Excerpt about Hetch Hetchy debates
  • Long Essay/DBQ: connect to the environmental movement of the 1960s–70s as a synthesis point
