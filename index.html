Worksheet Answers

1 Page 399-400. List and describe 3 different "tolls" on the environment from this era.

  • Destruction of Species: The grizzly bear, once found in large numbers from the Great Plains to California, was reduced to an estimated 800 by the early 20th century. The number of wolves declined from possibly 2 million to just 200,000, and by the mid-1880s, fewer than 5,000 buffalo survived in the entire U.S.
  • Soil Erosion and Dust Storms: The slaughter of the buffalo led ranchers and farmers to switch to cattle and sheep. Unlike buffalo, these livestock overgrazed the native grasses, destroying the ground cover. This caused the soil to erode and become barren, leading to huge dust storms sweeping across the plains by the end of the century.
  • Disappearance of Inland Waters: Large-scale irrigation for commercial agriculture rerouted and dammed water sources. For example, Lake Tulare in California's Central Valley, which once covered up to 760 square miles, shrank dramatically and eventually disappeared entirely as farmers tapped the rivers that fed it. The remaining land became so alkaline in spots that it was no longer usable for farming.

2 What were 2 separate government actions taken as a result of this?

  • Forest Reserve Act of 1891: This act gave the president the power to establish forest reserves to protect watersheds from threats like lumbering and overgrazing.
  • Newlands (National Reclamation) Act of 1902: This act provided federal funding for irrigation projects like dams and canals, aiming to turn arid land into productive farms.

3 Page 476. Who cares more about the environment...Theodore Roosevelt? Or John Muir? Why?
John Muir cared more about the intrinsic value of the environment. The text describes him as making a "passionate and spiritual defense of the inherent value of the American wilderness." He viewed nature, like the Hetch Hetchy Valley, as a "temple" that should be preserved for its own sake, not for commercial use. Roosevelt, while a conservationist, took the "middle ground between preservation and unrestricted commercial development," managing resources for public and economic good.

4 What would a conservationist say to a preservationist if they argued over land use?
A conservationist, like Gifford Pinchot, might say: "We must manage this land wisely for the greatest good of the greatest number of people over the long term. Developing this valley for a reservoir will provide clean drinking water and power for the city, which is a victory for the public good. This is about using our natural resources intelligently to benefit human society."
A preservationist, like John Muir, would reply: "This valley is not a resource to be exploited; it is a sacred temple of nature. Its value lies in its beauty and its wildness, not in its utility. To flood it for a city's needs is an act of destruction by the devotees of ravaging commercialism. It must be left untouched for its own sake and for the spiritual renewal of humanity."

5 21.6.5 What grade would you give Woodrow Wilson on his first term? What do you agree with or disagree with in his presidency?
Grade: C+
· What I agree with: I agree with his push for significant economic reforms like the Underwood-Simmons Act (reducing tariffs and creating a graduated income tax), the Federal Reserve Act (creating a more stable banking system), and the Clayton Antitrust Act (strengthening antitrust laws and protecting labor unions). He also later supported important social legislation like the Keating-Owen Act to ban child labor.
· What I disagree with: I strongly disagree with his racial policies. As a Southerner, he issued an executive order that instituted legal segregation in federal employment, forcing African Americans to work separately from white employees. This action was a major step backward for civil rights and contradicts the progressive ideals of justice and equality.

6 16th Amendment does what?
The Sixteenth Amendment gave Congress the power to levy a graduated tax on personal incomes.

8 22.2 Why did most American oppose U.S. involvement in WW1? 2 examples.

  • Strong Anti-War Movement: Groups like the Woman's Peace Parade and the American Union against Militarism, led by prominent progressives like Jane Addams, actively lobbied against military preparedness and intervention.
  • Political Opposition in Congress: A significant group of thirty to fifty House Democrats, led by majority leader Claude Kitchin, stubbornly opposed President Wilson's military buildup.

Crash Course Worksheet Answers

1 Problem and alternative, and how Jefferson/Hamilton apply in the 20th century:
The problem was that corporations were becoming national/international and controlling people's lives. The alternative was a strong national government. Herbert Croly argued that to achieve the Jeffersonian ideal of individual freedom, the country needed Hamiltonian government intervention in the economy.

2 Teddy's Square Deal aims AND federal government's responsibility:
The Square Deal aimed to distinguish good corporations (useful products at fair prices) from bad ones (existing only to profit). Roosevelt believed the federal government was responsible for regulating the economy directly and breaking up the power of wealthy corporations.

3 Actions Teddy took AND how Congress assisted:
Teddy used the Sherman Act to prosecute bad trusts (like Northern Securities Company) and appointed Gifford Pinchot to set aside millions of acres for national parks. Congress passed the Hepburn Act (1906), giving the ICC power to regulate railroad rates.

4 What Taft accomplished as a Progressive:
Taft ordered the prosecution that broke up Standard Oil (1911) and supported the 16th Amendment allowing a federal income tax.

5 Four candidates and platforms in 1912:

  • Taft (Republican) – conservative, limited trust-busting
  • Roosevelt (Bull Moose/Progressive) – New Nationalism; heavy taxes on wealth, federal regulation, welfare state programs
  • Debs (Socialist) – public ownership of railroads/banks, limits on work hours
  • Wilson (Democrat) – New Freedom; restore market competition, stronger anti-trust laws, support small business

6 Problems according to the Mystery Document (Wilson):
Excessive tariffs and near-universal monopoly, and that both wings of the Republican Party refused to fight these problems, accepting them instead through administrative commissions.

7 Four of Wilson's domestic accomplishments:

  • Underwood Tariff reducing import duties
  • Graduated income tax on the wealthiest 5%
  • Clayton Act (1914) exempting unions from antitrust laws
  • Keating-Owen Act outlawing child labor in manufacturing
  • (Also: Adamson Act — 8-hour workday for railroad workers; Federal Reserve System founded 1913)

8 The "contradiction" with Progressive presidents:
Progressive presidents were very concerned about big business threatening freedom at home, but in Latin America and the Caribbean they showed little concern for freedom at all, frequently intervening militarily to benefit American business interests.

9 What the Roosevelt Corollary empowered the U.S. to do AND how Teddy put it into practice:
It added to the Monroe Doctrine by declaring the U.S. would exercise "international police power" over the Western Hemisphere. In practice, Teddy used troops to seize the Dominican Republic's customs house to ensure debt repayment, and encouraged United Fruit Company investment in Honduras and Costa Rica, turning them into "banana republics."

10 Contrast Taft's foreign policy with Wilson's:
Taft used "Dollar Diplomacy" — emphasizing loans and economic investment rather than military force to spread American influence. Wilson, despite appearing more idealistic, actually intervened militarily in Latin America more than any other president, sending troops to Mexico to "teach" them democracy, though these interventions largely failed.

11 Foner Quote:
"The presidents who spoke the most about freedom were likely to intervene most frequently in the affairs of other countries."

Guided Notes (Conservation vs. Preservation)

Background Info

What is conservation? Advocating the proper use of nature and resources
- Example: Teddy Roosevelt / Gifford Pinchot — Roosevelt's chief forester

What is preservation? Advocating the protection of nature and land from humans
- Example: John Muir (Sierra Club)

Similarities Between The Two Groups

  • Both conservationists and preservationists advocated establishing national parks
  • Yellowstone in 1872 — 1st national park
  • Yosemite in 1890

Differences Between The Two Groups

  • Conservationists support using nature
  • Preservationists wanted to protect nature from human interaction

The Hetch Hetchy Controversy

  • Hetch Hetchy is in Yosemite National Park
  • San Francisco sought a stable water supply — proposed a dam
  • John Muir (Sierra Club and preservationist) opposed the dam
  • Pinchot (and Roosevelt) approved construction of the dam
    - 85% of water usage today in San Fran comes from Hetch Hetchy, 167 miles away

Test Tips

  • Multiple-Choice and Short Answer Questions:
    - Comparing and contrasting preservation and conservation
    - Excerpt about Hetch Hetchy debates
  • Long Essay/DBQ: connect to the environmental movement of the 1960s–70s as a synthesis point
